Actionable apprech to inexpensive solar energy in Europe

September 11, 2025 by Toni Vasile ],people

Despite not being the most efficient or cost-effective option—due to high installation costs, the need for inverters to integrate with AC systems, and diminishing returns at higher module efficiencies—solar technology remains one of the most versatile. It scales particularly well in large power-plant applications while still being efficient enough for individual use. In the latter, however, the greatest room for improvement remains. While multi-layer panels are approaching ~50% efficiency in highly optimized arrays, consumer panels still struggle to maintain around 20% efficiency at an economical price point. This is where lightweight options such as Building-Integrated Photovoltaics (BIPV)—driven by crystalline silicon and thin-film technologies like amorphous silicon (a-Si), cadmium telluride (CdTe), and copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS)—offer more flexible and cost-effective solutions. Emerging technologies such as perovskites are also gaining traction in Europe, where strong green initiatives and limited building space drive demand for affordable, versatile, and comparably efficient solar panels. Given current research and technological trends, this segment of the solar industry is projected to grow by 4% annually, from 704.1 GW in 2024 to 1,037.7 GW by 2034, driven heavily by demand in urban, commercial, and industrial rooftops using thin-film and lightweight systems.

A general comparrasion of photovoltaic and solar thermal

September 18, 2025 by Toni Vasile business,people

While photovoltaic cells suffer losses from not being able to fully utilize the full spectrum of incoming light, due to the Shockley–Queisser limit (33%) for single-layer cells. They can achieve a peak efficiency of around 50% for current top-of-the-line multi-layer cells. Though the theoretical efficiency limit to these multi-layer panels is currently assumed to be around 85% given infinite junction/concentration. Since these panels do not convert sunlight to mechanical energy from heat, they bypass the Carnot efficiency limit of heat engines used in concentrated solar power. Further losses in heat transfer and the electrical energy cost of initial startup for these plants bring real-world rates of concentrated solar down further to around the efficiency of multi-panel photovoltaics in high heat applications. Solar thermal heating technologies, on the other hand, do not convert the heat they generate to mechanical energy in this way. Therefore, the only energy loss it incurs is from heat transfer. Making solar thermal the most efficient on the market right now purely in the sense of how well it converts sunlight, rivaling even the theoretical efficiency of photovoltaics. However, this is just the theoretical physics aspect of these technologies, and many more real-world factors and aspects unique to each that influence their real-world use.
